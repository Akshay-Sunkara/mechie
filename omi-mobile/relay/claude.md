Where industrial maintenance has the edge over commercial HVAC
Pain density per customer. A single refinery has 200-800 maintenance techs, runs 24/7, and a single hour of unplanned downtime can be $50K-$500K. A commercial HVAC contractor has 30-150 techs and a missed service call costs maybe a few hundred dollars in callbacks. Regulatory teeth. Auto-documentation isn't a nice-to-have; it's a compliance artifact. HVAC has EPA 608 for refrigerant handling, but the documentation requirements are much lighter.
Knowledge transfer is mission-critical, not just inconvenient. The "Great Crew Change" in oil & gas and chemicals is real — a huge cohort of techs hired in the 70s and 80s retired in the 2010s-2020s, and replacing them is genuinely hard. Losing a 30-year refinery tech means losing fixes that aren't written down anywhere. In HVAC the same dynamic exists but the consequences are missed service calls, not a unit going down for a week.
Willingness to pay. Industrial maintenance budgets sit inside operations budgets that are already $millions per plant. HVAC contractors operate on 5-10% net margins and scrutinize every per-tech software dollar.
Specific pain points your wearable plausibly solves in industrial maintenance
The strongest ones, roughly in order of intensity:
Tribal knowledge capture before retirement. Senior tech walks the plant for 30 years, knows every quirk of Pump 47, retires. Wearable records sessions → searchable knowledge base. This is the #1 pain you'll hear from reliability managers.
CMMS work order data quality. Work order notes in SAP PM and Maximo are notoriously thin ("fixed pump, replaced seal"). Auto-population gives reliability engineers actual data to do failure analysis on.
Procedure compliance for safety-critical work — LOTO, confined space entry, hot work, line breaking. Real-time guidance ensures steps aren't skipped. A missed step can kill someone.
Time-to-productivity for apprentices. Industrial maintenance apprenticeships are 4-5 years. Anything that compresses that curve has real labor-cost ROI.
Mean time to repair. Junior tech gets stuck on an unfamiliar piece of equipment; guidance gets them unstuck faster. Direct downtime cost reduction.
Audit defense. When OSHA or EPA shows up, documented procedures and inspection records are the company's protection.
Where HVAC is actually better for you
I don't want to oversell industrial. For a freshman with no industry intros, HVAC has real advantages: faster sales cycles (weeks vs. 6-18 months), lower-level decision makers you can actually reach, easier MVP shipping (no certifications needed), and a known SaaS-buying market with proven integration partners (ServiceTitan, BuildOps).

Hazardous area certification. A standard wearable cannot be worn in these areas, period.
